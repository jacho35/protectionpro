## Critical

### [PROT-1] Critical — Arc-flash fuse clearing time hardcoded at 0.02 s regardless of arcing current
`backend/analysis/arcflash.py:353-356`

Code: `# Fuse typically clears in < 0.01s ... 20ms is a conservative estimate` → `upstream_times.append(0.02)`.
Should evaluate the fuse pre-arcing curve at the arcing current (IEEE 1584 §4.5/Annex); 20 ms only holds deep in the current-limiting region (≳10-20×In). Spot check: 0.4 kV bus, Ibf = 3 kA → Iarc = 2.19 kA (verified from Eq. 1); a gG 630 A upstream fuse melts in ~6-8 s at 2-2.2 kA per the app's own `FUSE_CURVES_GG` (8 s at 2 kA) → true clearing capped at 2 s, code uses 0.02 s → **incident energy understated ×100** (e.g. ~0.2 cal/cm² "no PPE" instead of ~20 cal/cm² Category 3). The comment's "conservative" claim is inverted — 20 ms is the *optimistic* end.
**Fix direction:** port `FUSE_CURVES_GG` to the backend and evaluate at Iarc, falling back to 2.0 s below the current-limiting threshold.

## High

### [EE-1] High — Zero-sequence walk loses transformer port orientation behind cables → phantom Z0 source seen from the delta side
`backend/analysis/fault.py:859`

The Z0 path enumerator forwards the entry port through transparent elements (`fault.py:867-869`: `walk(neighbor_id, z0_path, trail, remote_port, path_visited)`) but **drops it when traversing a cable**: line 858-859 reads `for neighbor_id, _, _ in adjacency.get(comp_id, []): walk(neighbor_id, z0_path + z_cable, new_trail, None, path_visited)`. A transformer reached with `entry_port=None` falls into the "port unknown" fallback (`fault.py:932-937`) which sets `bus_side_delta = False` and `bus_side_grounded = hv_grounded or lv_grounded`. For a Dyn11 unit viewed from its **delta (HV) side through a cable** — the most common MV topology — the fallback declares the transformer a Z0 source (grounded LV winding + "far-side delta"), when physically the delta winding blocks all zero-sequence current from the bus. Per IEC 60909-0 §6.3/Annex, Z(0) into a delta winding is infinite. Directly-wired transformers are handled correctly (port present → `bus_side='hv'` → blocked), so the bug appears only with an interposed cable, CB-cable chain, etc.

*Quantified:* 33 kV bus, 500 MVA utility (Z0=Z1), 1 km cable to a 10 MVA Dyn11 33/11 kV unit: phantom parallel Z0 ≈ 0.83 pu drops Z0 from 0.20 to 0.16 pu → Ik1 overstated ~7 %. On a resistance-earthed 11/33 kV system (utility Z0 dominated by an NER), the phantom solidly-grounded path can dominate entirely — reporting kiloamp earth-fault levels where the true current is NER-limited to a few hundred amps, corrupting earth-fault relay settings and every ik1-fed study (grounding GPR/mesh, SLG duty).
**Fix direction:** pass `remote_port` as `entry_port` in the cable branch exactly as the transparent-element branch does.

### [EE-2] High — DC arc flash omits the Stokes & Oppenlander current exponent; incident energy understated ~2.5-3×
`backend/analysis/dc_arcflash.py:123, 164, 200`

The code uses `v_arc = 20.0 + 0.534 * gap_mm` as the arc voltage. Stokes & Oppenlander (the method the module cites, and as applied in Ammerman et al. / IEEE 1584 DC guidance) give the arc **resistance** `R_arc = (20 + 0.534·z_g)/I^0.88`, i.e. arc voltage `V_arc = (20 + 0.534·z_g)·I^0.12` — the `I^0.12` term is dropped, so arc power `P = V_arc·I_arc` is computed with a V_arc valid only at I = 1 A.

*Quantified (hand calc):* 600 V DC, 20 kA bolted, gap 32 mm, t = 0.1 s, 455 mm: code gives V_arc = 37.1 V, I_arc = 18.8 kA, **E = 0.64 cal/cm² → "No PPE required"**. Iterative S&O solve: I_arc ≈ 16.0 kA, V_arc ≈ 119 V, **E = 1.75 cal/cm² → PPE Cat 1** (ratio 2.7×). Non-conservative on the safety side at all realistic DC fault levels; the deficit grows with current (I^0.12 ≈ 3.3 at 20 kA). Also note the model radiates into a full sphere with no enclosure focusing factor, further understating energy for enclosed DC gear (NFPA 70E Annex D.5 max-power method would give ~4× the code's energy here).
**Fix direction:** solve I_arc iteratively from `I = V_sys/(R_sys + (20+0.534G)/I^0.88)` and use `V_arc = I·R_arc`; consider a box multiplier for enclosed equipment.

### [PROT-2] High — Relay clearing time in arc flash is dead code, and would be wrong if reached
`backend/analysis/arcflash.py:357-360`, `frontend/js/constants.js:1215`

Code: `elif comp.type == "relay": tds = float(comp.props.get("tds", 1.0)); upstream_times.append(tds * 0.1 + 0.08)`. Three defects stack: (a) relays have `ports: []` in `COMPONENT_DEFS` — they are never wired, so they can never appear in the bus adjacency and this branch never executes; relay-protected MV buses instead get the placeholder VCB thermal-magnetic model (library `magnetic_pickup: 10` → inst threshold 6.3 kA on a 630 A VCB, else the 0.5/1.0/2.0 s bucket); (b) the property key is `time_dial` (constants.js:1224), not `tds`, so even a reachable branch would always use the 1.0 default; (c) `tds×0.1+0.08` is dimensionally unrelated to the IDMT curve — hand check: SI, TDS 0.5, M = 10 → t = 0.5×0.14/(10^0.02−1) = **1.49 s** vs heuristic **0.18 s** (~8× understated).
**Fix direction:** evaluate the relay's actual curve (`curve`, `pickup_a`, `time_dial`, `inst_pickup_a`) at Iarc, resolving relays via `associated_ct`/`trip_cb` since they are not in the wire graph.

### [EE-6 ≡ PROT-3] High — Arc-flash device search is one wire-hop from the bus → 2.0 s fallback in common layouts
`backend/analysis/arcflash.py:323-332`
*(found independently by both the EE and protection reviewers)*

Code: `neighbors = adjacency.get(bus.id, [])` then immediately `if comp.type not in ("cb","fuse","relay"): continue`. Any protective device separated from the bus by a cable, switch, or CT is invisible and the 2.0 s fallback applies. In the app's canonical layout `bus1 — CB — cable — bus2`, bus2 always gets 2.0 s even though the CB clears its fault; `utility — cable — CB — bus` misses the incomer breaker too. `_leads_to_source` already BFS-walks the whole graph for the upstream test — only the device *search* stops at one hop. Incident energy scales linearly with t: a fault actually cleared instantaneously in 50 ms is reported at **40× the energy**, pushing buses into Category 4/"DANGER" spuriously and driving the mitigation-recommendation loop (which keys off `t > 0.1/0.5 s`). Conservative in direction, but materially wrong results that drive real mitigation spend.
**Fix direction:** BFS from the bus toward the source (reusing `_leads_to_source` semantics / mirroring `duty_check._find_upstream_bus`'s transparent walk plus cables), taking the nearest device per infeed path.

### [PROT-4] High — Transformer damage curve uses the IEEE C57.109 Category I constant (1250·Ir²) for all sizes
`frontend/js/tcc.js:1732` and `:2838-2840`

Code: `const I2t = Ir * Ir * 1250;` (drawing) and `return (dev.ratedA*dev.ratedA*1250)/(currentA*currentA)` (trip-time). C57.109's t = 1250/I² applies to **Category I** (≤500 kVA) only. For Category II/III — most of the 22-entry library (100 kVA-80 MVA) — the through-fault duty at maximum through-fault current is ~2 s, i.e. K = (1/Z_pu)²·2. Hand check, Z = 6%: I_max = 16.7 pu, code plots t = 1250/16.7² = **4.5 s** at I_max vs the standard's **2 s** (K = 556 vs 1250 — withstand overstated 2.25×; at Z = 8% it is 4×). An engineer grading relay curves under the plotted curve can leave the transformer unprotected against through-faults it cannot actually withstand — unsafe and undetected.
**Fix direction:** select K per C57.109 category (from MVA), using the frequent-fault curve for Cat II/III.

### [PROT-5] High — A relay with `trip_cb` set but no `associated_ct` silently deletes its breaker from every grading path
`frontend/js/tcc.js:2905-2942`

Code: CBs in `cbsTrippedByRelay` are skipped from paths (`!cbsTrippedByRelay.has(node)`, line 2932), and the relay is re-inserted only at its CT's node (`dev.associated_ct === node`, line 2938). Relays are not wired (ports `[]`), so with `trip_cb` set and `associated_ct` empty, neither the CB nor the relay appears on any protection path — the pair vanishes from the coordination check, miscoordination detection, auto-grading, and sequence-of-operation, which then report "all pairs coordinated". Both properties are independent optional fields in the panel. Real miscoordination goes undetected for a plausible configuration.
**Fix direction:** when a relay has `trip_cb` but no CT, substitute the relay at the CB's node instead of dropping the CB.

### [PROT-6] High — Earth-fault (51N) elements are never graded against downstream phase-only devices
`frontend/js/tcc.js:2568-2570` with the class-skip at `:2714`, `:3011`, `:3413`

Code: `_deviceElementClass` returns `'earth'` only for `relayType === '50N/51N'`; fuses/CBs are always `'phase'`, and every engine returns early on class mismatch (`if (class(up) !== class(down)) return/continue`). But an earth fault downstream of a fuse flows through that fuse — standard practice grades the upstream 51N against the downstream fuse/CB at ik1. These pairs are silently skipped and reported coordinated. This is a blind spot introduced by the earlier "graded separately at ik3/ik1" fix. An upstream 51N that trips before a downstream fuse clears (nuisance feeder-wide outage on a spur earth fault) passes all checks.
**Fix direction:** grade earth-class *upstream* devices against phase-class *downstream* devices at ik1 (skip only the reverse pairing and phase-vs-earth same-position conflicts).

### [DEV-2] High — Cable-resistance migration inflates R by ×1.275 for v1 files that already hold operating-temperature values
`frontend/js/state.js:560-576`

```js

if (Math.abs(p.r_per_km - oldLibR) / oldLibR < 0.01) {  // only matches the OLD 20°C value
```

## Medium

### [EE-4] Medium — Utility source impedance omits the voltage factor c (IEC 60909-0 §6.2, Eq. 15)
`backend/analysis/fault.py:592-599`; mirrored at `frontend/js/properties.js:696` and `:1303`

`z_pu = base_mva / fault_mva`. IEC 60909-0 Eq. 15 defines the network-feeder equivalent as **Z_Q = c·U_nQ² / S″_kQ**, precisely so that computing I″k = c·U_n/(√3·Z_Q) at the connection point reproduces the declared I″_kQ. Omitting c and then applying c in the fault equation returns 1.1 × the declared fault level: entering "Fault Level 500 MVA at 11 kV" (I″kQ = 26.24 kA) yields Ik3 = 28.87 kA — and the regression test `test_ik3_infinite_bus` pins this deviant convention as correct. Overstates utility-dominated fault levels ~10 % (conservative for duty, but distorts arc-flash bolted current, TCC referral points, cable withstand sizing, and disagrees with the utility authority's declared level). Frontend and backend agree with each other, so this is a pure standards deviation, consistently displayed.
**Fix direction:** `z_pu = c · base_mva / fault_mva` (c per the bus voltage class), update the frontend derivation and the regression expectation together.

### [EE-5] Medium — Adiabatic withstand checks use Ik″ without the DC-heat factor (Ith = Ik″·√(m+n)); dead κ in grounding
`backend/analysis/cable_sizing.py:607-611`, `backend/analysis/grounding_system.py:299-311`

`min_size_for_fault = i_fault_a * math.sqrt(t_clear) / k` feeds the initial symmetrical current straight into k√t. IEC 60364-4-43/IEC 60949 sizing at short clearing times should use the thermal-equivalent current Ith = Ik″·√(m+n) (IEC 60909-0 §12): at κ ≈ 1.8, m = 0.66 at t = 0.02 s and 0.40 at 0.05 s — exactly the clearing times `_estimate_clearing_time` returns for fuses/MCCBs — so the required conductor area is **understated 18-29 %** (non-conservative). The same class of omission exists in grounding: `kappa` is read from the fault results (`grounding_system.py:302-307`) and then never used — no decrement factor D_f is applied to I_G (IEEE 80 Eq. 79: I_G = D_f·S_f·3I₀; D_f ≈ 1.03-1.1 for t_s ≤ 0.5 s), partially offset by the conservative S_f = 1.
**Fix direction:** multiply the fault current by √(m+n) in the cable check (m from κ and t) and apply D_f (or delete the dead κ and document S_f=1 as the compensating conservatism).

### [EE-7] Medium — Frontend SLG/LLG worked example sums impedance magnitudes and assumes Z2=Z1
`frontend/js/properties.js:922-925, 936-937`

The calc modal prints `Z_SLG = Z1 + Z2 + Z0 = 2×Z_eq + Z0` and evaluates it as `2 * zeqMag + busResult.z0_mag` — an arithmetic sum of magnitudes, whereas the engine sums complex impedances then takes |·| (`fault.py:116-117`), and it assumes Z2 = Z_eq even when a generator/motor `x2` makes Z2 ≠ Z1. When the Z0 angle differs from Z1 (resistance-earthed systems: Z0 nearly real, Z1 nearly reactive) the displayed intermediate arithmetic does not reproduce the reported I″k1 — an engineer checking the tool's own worked example will find it internally inconsistent (can diverge grossly, tens of %, exactly in NER cases; the final kA shown is the engine's correct value).
**Fix direction:** return z_slg components (or the complex sum) from the backend and display the complex-magnitude step.

### [PROT-7] Medium — Coordination test points are not capped at the downstream device's maximum through-fault
`frontend/js/tcc.js:2614-2645` used by `checkPair`/`detectMiscoordination`/`autoCoordinate`

Every bus's ik3/ik1 is referred to every in-series pair, including buses electrically upstream of (or not fed through) the pair. Example: an 11 kV bus point of 8 kA referred to a 0.4 kV pair evaluates it at 220 kA — a current the pair can never carry — producing false "race condition"/"insufficient margin" flags where both devices sit in their instantaneous regions, and driving `autoCoordinate` (same test points, line 2998) to raise upstream TDS to satisfy fictitious constraints, slowing real protection. Standard practice: grade each pair only up to the maximum through-fault at the downstream device.
**Fix direction:** restrict test points per pair to buses downstream of the pair's downstream device (the bus map from `_buildProtectionPaths(busMap)` already identifies source-side chains per bus).

### [PROT-8] Medium — Earth-fault currents referred across transformers by simple voltage ratio; vector group ignored
`frontend/js/tcc.js:2558-2561` with earth points from `:2623`

`_referCurrent` applies I×(V_fault/V_dev) to ik1 test points for earth-element pairs. Zero-sequence current does not propagate through a Dyn/YNd winding — an HV-side 51N sees no residual for an LV earth fault, yet the check grades HV-51N vs LV-51N pairs at the ratio-referred ik1 and can report either false miscoordination or false coordination.
**Fix direction:** for earth pairs, do not refer ik1 across delta-blocked windings (drop cross-transformer earth pairs unless the group passes zero sequence, e.g. YNyn).

### [PROT-9] Medium — Unbounded simple-path DFS in TCC path builders can hang the browser on meshed networks
`frontend/js/tcc.js:2920-2971` (`_buildProtectionPaths`) and `:3773-3809` (`_buildMiniSLDPaths`)

Both enumerate simple paths with per-branch `visited` copies and no expansion/path caps. The equivalent DFS in `components.js:49-50` was capped (MAX_PATHS 500 / MAX_EXPANSIONS 20000) in the prior fix round, and `fault.py` likewise — these two were missed. The coordination check (and hence these DFS runs) fires on every TCC open, tab click, visibility toggle, and drag-release. The app ships ring/mesh templates; a modest ring with parallel ties makes path count explode exponentially.

### [PROT-10] Medium — Focused grading ("View TCC Grading") omits relays from the upstream device set
`frontend/js/components.js:25-89` + `frontend/js/tcc.js:346`

`traceUpstreamProtection` collects only components physically on the wire path (`PROTECTION_TYPES` cb/fuse/relay + transformer/cable). Relays are never on wire paths (ports `[]`) and CTs are not mapped to their relays, so: opening grading from a CB shows no upstream relay curves, and opening it from a relay (context menu offers this, `contextmenu.js:103-108`) shows only the relay itself with zero upstream devices — the feature's core purpose defeated for relay-based schemes.
**Fix direction:** include CTs in the trace and add relays whose `associated_ct` or `trip_cb` lies on the path.

### [PROT-11] Medium — Arc-flash CB clearing ignores ACB short-time/instantaneous trip-unit settings
`backend/analysis/arcflash.py:334-352`

Only `trip_rating_a × magnetic_pickup` is used as the instantaneous threshold with a 0.05 s trip. An ACB configured ST-only (`short_time_pickup=6`, `short_time_delay=0.1-0.4 s`, `instantaneous_pickup=0` — a normal ZSI/selectivity setup) actually clears at the ST delay + breaker time (~0.15-0.45 s), yet the code returns 0.05 s once Iarc > 10×Ir → **energy understated ~3-9×**. Non-conservative direction.
**Fix direction:** mirror `cbTripTime`'s ACB priority (inst → ST → thermal) in `get_clearing_time`.

### [PROT-12] Medium — Fuse-upstream pairs get the same 0.2 s CTI as fuse-downstream pairs
`frontend/js/tcc.js:2578-2582`

Code: `if (fuses === 1) return 0.2;` regardless of which side the fuse is on. The 0.2 s relief ("no CB opening time on the fuse side") is valid only when the *fuse is downstream*. When the fuse is *upstream* of a relay/CB, its minimum-melting curve must clear the downstream relay time **plus** breaker opening (~40-80 ms) plus pre-arc tolerance/pre-damage allowance (~75% melting-time rule) — conventional margin ≥0.3-0.4 s. A 0.25 s actual margin passes here but can partially melt or blow the upstream fuse on every downstream fault.
**Fix direction:** return 0.2 s only when the downstream device is the fuse; use ≥ gradingMargin (or a melting-time derating) when the fuse is upstream.

### [PROT-13] Medium — No cable short-circuit withstand vs clearing-time check in compliance
`frontend/js/compliance.js` (absent; only In ≤ Iz at `:465-527`)

The compliance engine verifies overload protection (`In ≤ Iz`, SANS 10142-1 Cl. 5.5.2) but never verifies the adiabatic criterion of IEC 60364-4-43 §434.5.2 / SANS 10142-1 (t_clear ≤ k²S²/I²) even though every ingredient exists (fault results, `cbTripTime`/`fuseTripTime`, k-factors in `tcc.js:552-557`). A protected cable whose device clears a fault slower than its I²t limit passes the report.
**Fix direction:** add a check evaluating the upstream device's clearing time at the cable's through-fault current against k²S²/I².

## Overall assessment

The calculation basis is in substantially better shape than at the June audits: the per-unit machinery, sequence-network algebra, NR formulation, and the standards constants re-derived by hand are correct, the two-temperature cable-data discipline is coherently enforced, and the regression suite genuinely pins the previously fixed Criticals. The remaining defects cluster at the *edges* of the models rather than their cores — topology-dependent orientation loss (EE-1), a mis-transcribed empirical model in the newest engine (EE-2), device-tracing shortcuts in arc flash (PROT-1/2/3/11), and unfinished relabeling in the report layer (EE-3). The protection grading engines have correct curve math but several blind spots in *which pairs get graded at which currents*. On the software side, the recurring theme is fix batches that landed partially (name escaping, undo-clear, the 1584 relabel, DFS caps) — each missing one or two sites the original finding covered — which argues for the route-level and migration regression tests (DEV-12) as the structural remedy.
